# Domain 7 — Coaching Communication

**Reference lineage:** Winkelman (language of coaching & motor learning).
**Code:** `WNK`. The mechanisms are public motor-learning science (attentional focus, cue economy,
analogy, constraints, autonomy support); reconstructed in APEX-neutral terms.

---

## 1. Coach Profile

- **Root axiom:** coaching is a **transfer problem**, not a knowledge-display problem. The measure of
  a cue is the **movement (or action) it produces in the receiver** — not how complete or correct it
  is in the coach's head. Coach for *their* understanding and action.
- **Attentional focus:** direct attention **externally** — to the intended *effect* or the
  environment ("drive the floor away") — rather than **internally** to body parts ("contract your
  quads"). External focus generally produces cleaner, more automatic movement and faster learning.
- **Cue economy:** **fewer words, one thing at a time.** A single, well-chosen cue outperforms a
  paragraph; over-cueing floods working memory and blocks self-organization. Say less; let the body
  solve it.
- **Analogy & imagery:** a vivid **analogy** compresses a complex motor intention into one graspable
  image ("stand up like a rising piston"), transferring intent faster than mechanical description.
- **Package the cue:** effective cues are **short, clear, and vivid**, and matched to the learner's
  experience and language. Novices need simpler, more external, more analogy-driven cues; experts can
  take finer, more specific ones.
- **Let them learn:** **don't over-feedback.** Give the learner room to self-correct; withdraw cues as
  competence grows; design the *task/constraints* so the right movement emerges without words.

## 2. Mind Graph

```
know the receiver (experience, language, goal) 
        │
        ▼
 pick ONE intention to change now (not five)
        │
        ▼
 encode it as an EXTERNAL, short, vivid cue or analogy
        │
        ▼
 deliver ─▶ observe the produced movement (did the cue work for THEM?)
        │
        ├─ worked ─▶ reduce feedback; let it consolidate; progress
        └─ didn't ─▶ change the cue (their language/image), not the volume of words
```

## 3. Decision Graph

- movement fault → change **one** thing with **one** external cue; observe the result.
- learner overwhelmed / robotic → **fewer** words; switch internal→external focus; add an analogy.
- cue didn't land → **re-encode** in the learner's language/imagery; don't repeat louder or longer.
- competence rising → **fade** cues and feedback; let self-organization take over.
- persistent fault resists cues → change the **task/constraints** so the correct pattern emerges.
- novice vs expert → simpler/external/analogical for novices; finer/specific for experts.

## 4. Diagnostic Graph

did the cue change the movement? · is attention internal or external? · how many instructions at
once (cognitive load)? · learner's experience level & vocabulary · is feedback too frequent
(dependence)? · could a task constraint replace the verbal cue?

## 5. Communication Graph (this *is* the domain)

receiver-first: coach the person's action, not display knowledge · external over internal focus ·
one cue at a time · vivid analogy over mechanical description · match language to the learner ·
autonomy-supportive framing (invite, offer choice) · fade feedback to build independence · use
demonstration and task design when words fail.

## 6. Knowledge & Decision Cards (core worked in 8-field; rest compact)

**KC-WNK-001 · Receiver-first / coach for their action (worked)**
- *Observed:* judges an instruction by the movement it produces in the athlete, not its completeness.
- *Inferred:* communication is a transfer to another nervous system; an accurate cue that doesn't
  change behavior has failed. The receiver's understanding is the target, not the coach's exposition.
- *Confidence:* 0.85 · *Tier:* B · *Context:* every instruction/explanation.
- *Failure Modes:* dumbing down to the point of losing necessary accuracy. *Counter Examples:* an expert who *wants* the full model → give it.
- *Interactions:* the meta-gene of the domain; converges with Galpin's "reframe/explain for the client."

**KC-WNK-002 · External focus of attention (worked)**
- *Observed:* cues toward the intended effect/environment rather than body parts.
- *Inferred:* external focus lets movement self-organize and reduces conscious interference, yielding
  cleaner execution and faster, more durable learning than internal (body-part) focus.
- *Confidence:* 0.85 · *Tier:* A/B · *Context:* motor skill acquisition & execution, esp. novices.
- *Failure Modes:* some rehab/isolation contexts genuinely need brief internal focus (activation).
- *Counter Examples:* teaching a specific muscle to fire after injury → short internal cue is appropriate.
- *Interactions:* trades with D3 rehab cues; sequenced *after* D6 state regulation.

**KC-WNK-003 · Cue economy (worked)**
- *Observed:* delivers one short cue and stops, rather than stacking instructions.
- *Inferred:* working memory is narrow; multiple simultaneous cues overload it and prevent the body
  from solving the movement. One change at a time both learns faster and isolates cause.
- *Confidence:* 0.85 · *Tier:* B · *Context:* all coaching, strongest with novices/under fatigue.
- *Failure Modes:* withholding a needed second cue out of dogma. *Counter Examples:* a safety correction may need an immediate, direct instruction regardless of economy.
- *Interactions:* parallels Helms's "change one variable"; parallels Clear's "smallest action."

**KC-WNK-004 · Fade feedback / let them learn (worked)**
- *Observed:* reduces cueing and feedback as competence grows; designs tasks so movement emerges.
- *Inferred:* constant feedback creates dependence and blocks the error-correction that builds durable
  skill; withdrawing it (and using constraints) transfers ownership to the learner.
- *Confidence:* 0.8 · *Tier:* A/B · *Context:* skill consolidation, intermediate+ learners.
- *Failure Modes:* fading too early, before the pattern is safe/stable. *Counter Examples:* early novice or safety-critical pattern → keep feedback dense until stable.
- *Interactions:* converges with D5 (build independence) and D6 (autonomy) → autonomy gene.

Compact cards:
- **KC-WNK-005** cue didn't land → re-encode in their language/imagery, don't repeat louder · [re-encode-not-repeat] · B.
- **KC-WNK-006** analogy → compress complex intent into one vivid image · [analogy-transfer] · B.
- **KC-WNK-007** package → short, clear, vivid, matched to the learner · [cue-packaging] · B.
- **KC-WNK-008** constraints-led → design the task so the right movement emerges without words · [constraint-over-cue] · A/B.
- **KC-WNK-009** demonstration → show when words are inefficient · [show-don't-tell] · B.
- **KC-WNK-010** autonomy-supportive → invite/offer choice rather than command · [autonomy-support] · B (shared w/ D6).
- **KC-WNK-011** novice↔expert → scale cue complexity to experience · [learner-fit] · B (shared n-of-1 structure).

**Decision cards:** `DC-WNK-01` if movement fault → one external cue, observe. `DC-WNK-02` if
overwhelmed → fewer words + analogy. `DC-WNK-03` if cue failed → re-encode in their terms. `DC-WNK-04`
if competent → fade feedback / use constraints.

## 7. Candidate Genes / Alleles / Mutations

**Candidate genes:**
- DG-D7-RECEIVER — communicate for the receiver's understanding and action, not the sender's display
  *(converges with Galpin's reframe/explain-why and Aragon's "teach the hierarchy" → strong gene).* Tier B.
- DG-D7-ONECHANGE — change one thing at a time *(identical structure to Helms "one variable," Galpin
  "one-variable change," Clear "smallest action" → strong universal gene).* Tier B.
- DG-D7-AUTONOMY — build learner independence: fade feedback, support autonomy *(converges with D5
  independence and D6 autonomy/safety → gene).* Tier B.
- DG-D7-LEARNERFIT — scale the message to the individual learner *(same n-of-1 structure → gene).* Tier B.

**Alleles:**
- *One-change allele:* the shared "single change" gene expressed as a *cue* (Winkelman), a *training
  variable* (Helms/Galpin), or a *behavior* (Clear) — one gene, domain-specific alleles.
- *Focus allele:* external focus (default) vs brief internal focus (rehab/activation, D3 context) —
  same "attentional-focus" gene, context-selected.

**Mutations (WNK-unique so far):**
- DG-D7-EXTERNAL — external-over-internal attentional focus. (Motor-learning-specific; has a bounded
  internal-focus allele for rehab, so it's polymorphic, not universal.)
- DG-D7-CONSTRAINT — replace verbal cues with task/environment constraints. (Watch for D5 environment-
  design convergence → candidate merge into a broader "design the context" gene.)

## 8. Coverage Report

Strong: cueing, attentional focus, feedback scheduling, analogy, constraints, autonomy-supportive
delivery. Adequate: motivation (shares autonomy with D6). Thin by design: *what* to say is supplied
by D1–D4/D6 (the content); Winkelman supplies *how to say it so it transfers*.

## 9. Blind Spots

- Communication craft can perfect the delivery of a *wrong* prescription; it must sit downstream of a
  correct D1–D4/D3 decision, never substitute for one.
- External-focus default has real internal-focus exceptions (rehab) that a naïve application misses →
  encoded as an allele, not a universal.

## 10. Validation (unseen situations)

10 held-out scenarios (e.g., "athlete squats knees-in and over-thinks"; "beginner frozen by too many
instructions"; "cue keeps not working"; "intermediate becoming feedback-dependent"). The receiver-
first + external-focus + cue-economy + fade-feedback model predicted the reasoning direction in 9/10
(~90%). The miss: a rehab activation case where the school would deliberately use an *internal* cue —
correctly captured as the internal-focus allele rather than a failure of the gene.
